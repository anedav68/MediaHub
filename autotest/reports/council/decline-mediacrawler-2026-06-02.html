Should MediaHub integrate MediaCrawler?

VERDICT — DECLINE (unanimous, 5 / 5)
The question. Where would integrating NanmiCoder/MediaCrawler be helpful — and should it be integrated at all? MediaCrawler is a Python + Playwright crawler that keeps logged-in sessions to scrape 7 Chinese platforms only (Xiaohongshu, Douyin, Kuaishou, Bilibili, Weibo, Tieba, Zhihu), under a custom "Non-Commercial Learning License 1.1" that prohibits commercial use. MediaHub is a commercial UK/EU swimming-club SaaS (Instagram/Facebook/TikTok) whose moat is being explainable, auditable, GDPR- clean and human-approved — and which already has a polite brand-voice learner (brand/link_learners) and a human-gated publishing/ subsystem.

Where the council agrees

  • License is disqualifying — non-commercial licence cannot ship in a paid SaaS (identical to the 9router decline).
  • Platform mismatch is total — 7 Chinese platforms vs UK/EU clubs on Instagram/Facebook/TikTok/X. Zero overlap.
  • The one plausible use is already solved, better — brand/link_learners learns club voice via polite public reads (og:meta / oembed), GDPR-safe and auditable.
  • It contradicts the moat — logged-in anti-bot crawling is "proper ingestion, never evasion" (the CloakBrowser verdict) all over again.

Where the council clashes

  • Category error vs compliance failure — First Principles says MediaCrawler solves a non-problem; the others lead with license/ToS/platform. Both right, complementary.
  • "Not even a council question" — the Contrarian's "just existing policy" was flagged as circular: the council is what confirms the 9router/CloakBrowser reasoning transfers (it does).
  • Nuance: MediaHub does read public pages politely — the real line is polite public read vs logged-in session scrape, not crawl vs no-crawl.

Blind spots the peer review caught

  • Supply-chain / dependency hygiene (unanimous new point). Even vendoring or listing MediaCrawler — never calling it — drags session-persistence / anti-detection code and transitive deps into the production container, creating audit & ToS exposure by association. Prohibit listing it even as an optional/dev dep; candidate for a CI / SBOM guard.
  • Need a durable principle, not "no need today," so a future "benchmarking/trend-scraping" ask doesn't reopen it.
  • The safest "learn our voice" supply is consensual — club-uploaded exemplars (exemplar_post) moot crawling entirely.

The recommendation

Decline. Do not integrate, vendor, or list MediaCrawler in any form, now or "for later." Record it as an ADR in the CloakBrowser/9router series. Grow the polite brand/link_learners path or accept club-uploaded exemplars for any future "learn our voice" need — never a logged-in / anti-bot crawler.

The one thing to do first. Write docs/adr/0002-decline-mediacrawler-integration.md (done), and open a follow-up to add a CI dependency-policy / SBOM check that blocks non-commercial-licensed and anti-detection packages from the dependency graph — the same guard the CloakBrowser ADR already left as a follow-up.

Full advisor responses

The Contrarian — three kill shots
A dead deal. (1) The license is an absolute bar — "Non-Commercial Learning License 1.1" explicitly prohibits commercial use; MediaHub charges money; no grey zone, identical to the 9router decline. (2) Platform mismatch is total — zero of MediaHub's platforms; you cannot carve a use case from a tool that serves none of your use cases. (3) brand/link_learners already exists and is architecturally superior — polite, oembed/meta-based, GDPR-safe, ToS-compliant, auditable. A crawler that "bypasses anti-bot defences" contradicts the GDPR-cleanliness and auditability that justify the pricing premium. Decline. Write the ADR.
First Principles — the wrong question
"Should MediaHub integrate MediaCrawler?" is the wrong question. What problem does MediaHub actually have? Its moat is structured data in → intelligence → branded content out; the club provides results files; it never needed to crawl. MediaCrawler adds Chinese platforms + logged-in scraping — the antithesis of the GDPR-clean, auditable, source-grounded promise. The "brand voice learning" use is already solved by link_learners; no gap. The trap is integration-for-integration's-sake — its social interaction is publishing, not scraping. Decline.
The Expansionist — bull case is zero
Finding upside is my job, and finding none is itself a strong signal. Seven Chinese platforms vs UK/EU clubs — no overlap. Getting value would mean pivoting the whole customer base to Chinese platforms: a different company. The one angle I looked for — could CDP/session-persistence patterns inform link_learners? No; that design is deliberately polite, and importing evasion patterns undermines the auditability moat. The non-commercial license forecloses any commercial bull case. Decline; no carve-out.
The Outsider — fresh eyes
A tool that secretly logs into Chinese platforms as a fake user to scrape data those platforms don't want scraped — and whose license says don't use it commercially. MediaHub's pitch is being above-board, GDPR-compliant, human-approved. Secret logged-in crawling makes them the opposite. No UK/EU swim club needs Xiaohongshu data. "Narrow carve-out" is incoherent — you can't "narrowly" log into someone's account without permission. Commercial use of a non-commercial tool is illegal use, not a gray area. It's bolting a shoplifting device onto an ethical product. Decline cleanly.
The Executor — Monday morning
The path dead-ends before step 3. Step 1: install — you immediately hit the Non-Commercial license; commercial SaaS; over. Step 2 (ignore license): seven Chinese platforms vs Instagram/Facebook/TikTok/X — no shared target. Step 3 (ignore both): logged-in CDP sessions vs polite og:meta reads — swapping a compliant reader for a heavy session manager that violates Western ToS and needs persistent server-side cookies across multi-tenant runs: an IDOR / credential-leak nightmare. Decline. The real need is already met by link_learners.

Peer-review highlights

What the reviewers concluded

Strongest responses split between B (Outsider) — names the moral contradiction precisely and closes the "carve-out" as incoherent — and E (First Principles) — reframes it as a category error, not just a policy violation.

Biggest blind spots: C (Contrarian)'s "not even a council question" (circular — the council is what confirms the policy transfers), and E's overshoot ("MediaHub never crawls" — it does read public pages politely; the real line is polite-read vs logged-in-scrape).

What all five missed (unanimous): supply-chain / dependency-hygiene risk — even an uncalled, "reference-only" dependency puts anti-detection code and transitive deps into the container and the audit surface. Plus: the decline needs a durable cross-platform principle, and the safest voice-supply is consensual club uploads.
